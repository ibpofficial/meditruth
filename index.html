The Prescription Trap: How Profit Overrides Patient Care Rajesh, a 45-year-old schoolteacher from a small town in Uttar Pradesh, clutched his stomach in pain as he sat in the crowded waiting room of a well-known doctor. After hours of waiting, he was finally called in. The doctor barely looked up as Rajesh described his symptoms—burning pain in his abdomen, acidity, and nausea. Without much examination, the doctor scribbled on his prescription pad and handed it over. "Take this medicine twice a day for two weeks. It’s the best for your condition," the doctor said. Rajesh glanced at the prescription—"Pantop-DSR"—a branded medicine costing ₹350 for a strip of 10 tablets. He hesitated. His salary was modest, and he had a family to support. But the doctor assured him it was necessary.

The Hidden Truth What Rajesh didn’t know was that Pantoprazole + Domperidone (the same salt combination) was available in generic form for just ₹50–₹80 per strip. The doctor, however, had a tie-up with the pharmaceutical company, earning a commission for every branded prescription he wrote. When Rajesh asked the chemist for a cheaper alternative, the man shrugged. "Doctor ne yehi likha hai, isko hi lo." (The doctor has prescribed only this, take only this.) Why Patients Suffer Lack of Awareness – Most patients, especially in rural and semi-urban areas, don’t know that generic medicines (with the same salt composition) exist at a fraction of the cost. Fear & Trust – Patients believe doctors always prescribe what’s best for them, not realizing that brand loyalty is often driven by incentives. Chemist Complicity – Many pharmacists refuse to substitute prescribed brands, either due to pressure from doctors or their own profit margins. No Regulation – While India has Jan Aushadhi Kendras (government-approved generic medicine stores), doctors rarely recommend them, and awareness is low.
